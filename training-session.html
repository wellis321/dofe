Training Plan

Session Structure (10-12 minutes total)

Note: The housekeeping section ensures all participants can engage, which may add 1-2 minutes to the total time. This is essential for an effective training session.

  1. Housekeeping & Introductions (1.5 minutes)

    Before we begin the training, I'll do a brief housekeeping section to ensure everyone is comfortable with the Teams interface and can participate effectively:

    • Introductions - Brief introduction of myself and the purpose of the session
    • Who we are and why we're here - Explain the context: this is part of the interview process, demonstrating training delivery skills
    • Teams Chat Function - Show them where the chat function is in Teams and ask them to open it
    • Chat Monitoring - Let them know I'll be keeping an eye on the chat for any questions throughout the session, and they can do the same
    • Raise Hand Feature - Demonstrate how to raise a hand in Teams, then ask them to practice raising their hand so I know they can use it
    • Chat Practice - Ask them to type "Hello. It's [weather description]" in the chat (e.g., "Hello. It's rainy" or "Hello. It's sunny") so I can confirm they can use the chat function before we begin

    Why this matters: This ensures all participants can engage and ask questions during the session, making the training more interactive and accessible.

  2. Introduction (1 minute)

    "Good morning! Today I'm going to show you how to log into Twitter - a social media platform where people share thoughts, news, and connect with others. Even if you've never used social media before, by the end of these 10 minutes, you'll understand exactly how to access Twitter safely and what to do if something goes wrong."

  3. What is Twitter & Why Log In? (1.5 minutes)
    • Twitter is like a digital noticeboard where people share short messages
    • You need an account (like having a library card)
    • Logging in proves it's really you accessing your account
    • Brief mention: available on phones, tablets, computers
  4. The Login Process - Step by Step (4 minutes)
    • Step 1: Finding Twitter - Open web browser or app store, type "Twitter" in search, click the official Twitter
    • Step 2: The Login Screen - Identify the two boxes: Username/Email and Password
    • Step 3: Entering Your Details - Demonstrate typing, explain password dots/asterisks, show the "show password" eye icon option
    • Step 4: Clicking Log In - What happens next (loading, then your feed appears)
  5. Common Problems & Solutions (2.5 minutes)
    • Problem 1: "Forgot Password" - Where to find the link, what happens when you click it, checking email for reset link
    • Problem 2: "Incorrect Username or Password" - Check caps lock, check spelling, try typing in a notes app first
    • Problem 3: "Suspicious Login Attempt" - This is Twitter protecting you, how to verify it's really you (code to phone/email)
    • Problem 4: "Account Disabled" - What this means, how to appeal/contact support
  6. Top Tips for Safe Login (1 minute)
    • Never share your password with anyone
    • Use a strong password (mix of letters, numbers, symbols)
    • Log out on shared devices
    • Be suspicious of emails asking for your password
    • Enable two-factor authentication (brief explanation)
  7. Summary & Questions (30 seconds)

    "Today we've covered how to log into Twitter, what to expect, and how to solve the most common problems. Remember: everyone was a beginner once, and it's okay to go slowly."

SLIDE 2: WHAT IS TWITTER?

Visual Elements: Large Twitter logo at top, 3 simple icons with text: [Message Icon] "Share Thoughts & News", [People Icon] "Connect with People", [Globe Icon] "Stay Informed"

Speaker Notes: "So, what is Twitter? Think of it as a digital noticeboard where people share short messages, called 'tweets.' People use Twitter to share thoughts, read news, follow topics they're interested in, and keep in touch with people they care about. It's like a conversation happening in real-time. Twitter is used by millions of people around the world - from journalists sharing breaking news, to businesses connecting with customers, to friends sharing what they're up to."

Timing: 45 seconds

SLIDE 3: WHY DO YOU NEED TO LOG IN?

Visual Elements: Icon of a key or padlock [Lock Icon], Simple diagram showing: "Your Account" â†’ "Login" â†’ "Access Your Tweets & Connections"

Speaker Notes: "Before you can use Twitter, you need to 'log in.' But what does that mean? Logging in is like unlocking a door - it proves to Twitter that you're really you, and not someone else trying to access your account. You need a Twitter account first - think of it like having a library card. Once you have an account, logging in is how you access it each time. This keeps your tweets, messages, and connections private and secure. You only need to log in once on each device, and Twitter will usually remember you after that."

Timing: 1 minute

SLIDE 4: WHERE TO FIND TWITTER

Visual Elements: Split screen design - Left: "On a Phone or Tablet" - Screenshot of App Store with Twitter in search, Right: "On a Computer" - Screenshot of web browser with twitter.com

Speaker Notes: "Let me show you how to find Twitter. If you're using a phone or tablet, you'll need to download the Twitter app first. Open your app store - that's the 'App Store' on an iPhone or iPad, or 'Play Store' on an Android device. Type 'Twitter' in the search bar, and you'll see the Twitter app with its blue bird logo. Make sure it's the official one - you'll see a verified checkmark next to it. Tap to download and install it. If you're using a computer, it's even simpler. Open your web browser - that's Chrome, Safari, Firefox, or Edge - and type 'twitter.com' or 'x.com' in the address bar at the top. Press Enter, and you'll be taken to Twitter's website."

Timing: 1 minute 30 seconds

SLIDE 5: THE LOGIN SCREEN EXPLAINED

Visual Elements: Large, annotated screenshot of Twitter login screen with numbered arrows pointing to: 1. "Username or Email" box, 2. "Password" box, 3. "Show Password" eye icon, 4. "Log In" button, 5. "Forgot Password?" link

Speaker Notes: "Now, let's look at the login screen - this is what you'll see when you open Twitter. There are two main boxes here. The first box asks for your 'Username or Email.' This is either the username you chose when you created your account, or the email address you used to sign up. The second box is for your password. When you type your password, you'll see dots or asterisks instead of the actual letters. Don't worry - this is completely normal! Twitter does this to keep your password private. See this little eye icon? If you click on that, it will show you what you're typing. This is really useful to make sure you've typed your password correctly. Finally, there's the 'Log In' button. Once you've filled in both boxes, you'll click this to log in."

Timing: 1 minute 30 seconds

SLIDE 6: STEP-BY-STEP LOGIN DEMONSTRATION

Visual Elements: 4-step visual process flow with icons and arrows

LIVE DEMONSTRATION ELEMENT: Switch to screen share of actual Twitter login page or show pre-recorded video

Speaker Notes: "Let me walk you through this step by step. Step 1: Click on the first box and carefully type your username or email address. Step 2: Click on the password box and type your password. Notice how it shows dots instead of letters? That's keeping your password secure. Step 3: Now I'll click the 'Log In' button. Step 4: Twitter is now checking your details... and there we go! We're in. You'll see your Twitter feed appear - that's your home page with tweets from people you follow. It really is that simple - username, password, click Log In, and you're in!"

Timing: 2 minutes

SLIDE 7: COMMON PROBLEM #1 - FORGOT PASSWORD

Visual Elements: Red "PROBLEM" header, Screenshot showing error message, Green "SOLUTION" header, Visual flow diagram showing the password reset process

Speaker Notes: "Now, let's talk about the most common problems people face, and how to solve them. The number one issue? 'I've forgotten my password!' Don't worry - this happens to everyone, and it's easy to fix. Here's what you do: On the login screen, you'll see small text that says 'Forgot Password?' Click on that. Twitter will ask for your email address or username. Type it in and click 'Send Login Link.' Now, check your email inbox. You should see an email from Twitter with a link. Click on that link - it will take you to a page where you can create a brand new password. Choose a password you'll remember, but make it secure. A good tip is to use a phrase you'll remember, like 'BlueCats2025!' - it's got letters, numbers, and a symbol, which makes it strong. Write it down somewhere safe if you need to."

Timing: 1 minute 15 seconds

SLIDE 8: COMMON PROBLEM #2 - INCORRECT USERNAME/PASSWORD

Visual Elements: Split design showing two common issues: Left side shows "Incorrect username or password" with checklist, Right side shows "TIP: Use the eye icon"

Speaker Notes: "Another common problem: you type in your details, click Log In, and Twitter says 'Your username or password is incorrect.' Frustrating, right? But there are a few simple things to check. First, look at your keyboard. Is the Caps Lock key on? Passwords are case-sensitive, which means 'Password' and 'password' are considered different. If Caps Lock is on, turn it off and try again. Second, check your spelling carefully. It's easy to accidentally add an extra letter or miss one out. Third, check for spaces. Sometimes we accidentally press the space bar before or after typing. There shouldn't be any spaces before or after your username or password. Here's a great tip: click that eye icon I showed you earlier. This lets you see what you're actually typing, so you can spot any mistakes."

Timing: 1 minute

SLIDE 9: COMMON PROBLEM #3 - SECURITY CODE REQUEST

Visual Elements: Screenshot showing security code entry screen, Large "This is a GOOD thing! [Shield Icon]" message, Visual flow showing the verification process

Speaker Notes: "Sometimes, Twitter will ask you for a security code after you've entered your password. You might think 'Oh no, what's wrong?' But actually, this is a good thing! It means Twitter is protecting your account. Twitter sends you a security code when you log in from a new device or a new location. It's Twitter's way of checking that it's really you trying to log in, and not someone else who has guessed your password. Here's what to do: Check your phone for a text message, or check your email inbox. You'll receive a 6-digit code - it'll look something like '123 456.' Type this code carefully into Twitter, then click 'Confirm.' That's it! You'll be logged in, and Twitter now knows this device is safe for you to use. You usually won't need to do this again on the same device."

Timing: 1 minute

SLIDE 10: TOP 5 SAFETY TIPS

Visual Elements: Large heading: "Stay Safe on Twitter", 5 boxes, each with icon and tip

Speaker Notes: "Before we finish, let me share five crucial safety tips. These will help keep your Twitter account secure. Tip number one, and this is the most important: Never, ever share your password with anyone. Not your friends, not your family, not even someone claiming to be from Twitter. Your password is like the key to your house - you wouldn't give that to a stranger! Tip two: Use a strong password. Mix up letters, numbers, and symbols. Instead of 'password123,' try something like 'BlueCats2025!' - it's much harder for someone to guess. Tip three: If you're using a shared computer - like at a library or a friend's house - always remember to log out when you're finished. Tip four: Be very suspicious of emails asking for your Twitter password. Twitter will never, ever ask for your password by email. If you get an email like this, it's a scam - delete it immediately. And tip five: Trust your gut. If something feels suspicious or too good to be true, it probably is."

Timing: 1 minute 30 seconds

SLIDE 11: SUMMARY & NEXT STEPS

Visual Elements: Checkmark list of what was covered, "Remember" box, "Need More Help?" section

Speaker Notes: "And that's it! We've covered everything you need to know to log into Twitter. Let's quickly recap: We learned what Twitter is - a place to share thoughts and connect with people. We found out how to locate Twitter on your device or computer. We went through the step-by-step login process - username, password, click Log In. We looked at the three most common problems people face and exactly how to fix them. And we covered five essential safety tips to keep your account secure. But here's the most important thing I want you to remember: Everyone was a beginner once. Absolutely everyone. So please, don't feel pressured to remember everything perfectly. Take your time, be patient with yourself, and don't be afraid to ask for help."

Timing: 1 minute 30 seconds

Design Guidelines

  • Colors: High contrast combinations, suggested palette: Deep blue, Bright teal, Warm orange, White or very light gray background, Dark gray text
  • Typography: Headings: Bold, 36-44pt, Body text: 24-28pt (minimum), Never go below 20pt, Use sans-serif fonts
  • Layout: Generous white space, Maximum 3-4 bullet points per slide, Maximum 6-7 words per bullet point, Left-align text, Use images and icons liberally
  • Accessibility: Describe visual elements verbally, Don't rely solely on color to convey meaning, Provide alt text for all images, Test with grayscale filter

Emergency Protocols

If Internet Fails:

  1. Stay calm - tell them "I'm experiencing a connection issue"
  2. Try phone hotspot immediately
  3. If that fails: "Can we reschedule? I'm having technical difficulties."
  4. Follow up immediately with email explaining situation

If You Blank on Content:

  1. Take a breath
  2. Look at your notes (that's why they're there!)
  3. Say: "Let me clarify that point..."
  4. Refer to your slides as prompts
  5. Keep going - everyone blanks sometimes

If Demo Doesn't Work:

  1. Switch to screenshots immediately
  2. Say: "I have prepared screenshots in case of technical issues"
  3. This shows foresight and preparation!
  4. Continue smoothly
