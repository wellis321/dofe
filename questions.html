The DofE is focused on modernising its digital platforms, such as eDofE. How would you approach migrating legacy documentation into a modern knowledge management framework? What steps would you take to ensure data integrity, consistency, and usability?

Modern migration approach:

Content audit and inventory

Start by cataloguing all existing documentation, identifying what's current, outdated, duplicated, or missing. Use analytics and user feedback to prioritise high-value content.

User journey mapping

Map documentation to actual user workflows and tasks. Identify pain points where users struggle to find information or complete tasks.

Modern platform selection

Choose platforms that support collaboration, version control, searchability, and accessibility. Consider cloud-based knowledge bases (SharePoint, Confluence, GitBook) that integrate with existing tools like Microsoft 365.

Structured content approach

Move from unstructured documents to structured, modular content. Use templates, taxonomies, and metadata to make content reusable and easier to maintain.

Incremental migration

Use a phased approach: start with high-traffic, high-value content. Migrate in waves rather than all at once to minimise disruption.

Real Example: Consolidating Distributed Documentation

Context: I understand that eDofE currently has distributed documentation - various Licensed Organisations (schools, local authorities, youth groups) create their own PDF guides for participants, like the Gold Award guide from JGHS. This creates inconsistency and accessibility issues.

Action: I would apply my "Everybody First" design system to create centralised, accessible documentation that Licensed Organisations could customise while maintaining core consistency. Start with the most accessible version as the foundation, allowing organisations to add their branding while ensuring all users get a fully functional experience.

Outcome: This would eliminate the current fragmented approach, ensure WCAG compliance across all documentation, and provide DofE's diverse volunteer network with consistent, accessible information regardless of their Licensed Organisation or technical abilities.

Metadata and tagging

Add consistent metadata, tags, and categories to improve searchability and content discovery. Use controlled vocabularies for consistency.

Co-create templates and standards

Work with stakeholders to develop templates, style guides, and governance frameworks. Ensure buy-in from content creators and maintainers.

Automation and tooling

Use scripts or tools to automate bulk conversions, link checking, and format standardisation where possible. Leverage AI tools for content analysis and suggestions.

Search and discovery

Implement robust search functionality with filters, faceted search, and related content suggestions. Ensure content is discoverable through multiple entry points.

Governance and maintenance

Establish clear ownership, review cycles, and update processes. Set up workflows for content creation, approval, and archiving.

User testing and feedback loops

Test the new framework with real users, gather feedback, and iterate. Monitor usage analytics to identify gaps or areas needing improvement.

Training and change management

Provide training on the new platform and documentation standards. Support staff through the transition with clear communication and resources.

Ensuring data integrity, consistency, and usability:

Data integrity

Implement validation checks during migration, verify all links and references, maintain version control, and create backup/rollback procedures. Use automated tools to detect broken links, missing files, and corrupted data.

Consistency

Establish style guides and templates before migration. Use controlled vocabularies and metadata standards. Implement automated checks for formatting, terminology, and structure consistency across all migrated content.

Usability

Conduct user testing throughout the migration process. Ensure content is accessible (WCAG compliant), searchable, and logically organised. Provide clear navigation, search functionality, and user-friendly interfaces that match how staff actually work.

Key principles: Focus on user needs, maintain content quality during migration, ensure accessibility from day one, and establish sustainable processes for ongoing maintenance. The goal is not just moving content, but transforming it into a more usable, discoverable, and maintainable knowledge resource that supports DofE's mission of modernising digital platforms like eDofE.

When training staff with varying levels of IT proficiency, how do you ensure the sessions are engaging, accessible, and effective for everyone? Please include an example.

Real Example: Microsoft 365 Training for Mixed-Ability Groups

Context: I was delivering Microsoft 365 training to a group of 25 staff members at a healthcare organisation, ranging from complete beginners who rarely used computers to advanced users who were frustrated with basic content.

Action: I used my "favourite song" technique - when sending out the agenda, I asked everyone to reply with their favourite song. On training day, I played these songs from a Spotify playlist during breaks and morning setup. When participants heard their song, they got genuinely excited and it created instant personal connection.

Outcome: This simple technique broke down barriers immediately. The advanced users became mentors to beginners, everyone felt personally valued, and the relaxed atmosphere made it safe for people to ask questions regardless of their skill level.

Differentiated Learning Pathways

Context: During the same session, I needed to ensure both beginners and advanced users stayed engaged with SharePoint training.

Action: I created three learning pathways: "Getting Started" (basic navigation), "Building Confidence" (creating and sharing documents), and "Power User" (advanced collaboration features). Participants could choose their pathway and move between them as needed.

Outcome: Everyone progressed at their own pace, advanced users helped beginners, and by the end, even the most hesitant participants were confidently using SharePoint for their daily work.

"Everybody First" Design Philosophy in Training

Context: I apply my "Everybody First" design system to training delivery - instead of creating standard content and then adding accessibility features, I start with the most accessible version as the foundation.

Action: All my training materials begin with clear structure, plain language, multiple formats, and full keyboard/screen reader compatibility. Participants who want additional visual styling or complexity can access enhanced versions, but everyone gets a fully functional, fully styled from the start.

Outcome: This approach meensures accessibility isn't a special accommodation - it's the foundation that benefits everyone. Participants consistently feedback that this approach makes learning easier and more inclusive, regardless of their abilities or technical proficiency.

Describe a time when a trainee or group struggled to grasp a system or concept during your training. How did you identify the issue, and what steps did you take to resolve it?

Real Example: SharePoint Training Breakthrough

Context: During a Microsoft 365 training session for a local authority, I noticed three participants (all over 50) who had been enthusiastic during the morning session became increasingly quiet and withdrawn when we moved to SharePoint document libraries.

Action: I identified the issue by observing their body language - they'd stopped taking notes, weren't attempting the exercises, and kept looking at each other with frustrated expressions. During the break, I approached them individually and discovered they were overwhelmed by the interface and felt "stupid" compared to younger colleagues.

Outcome: I immediately adapted my approach: I related SharePoint to filing cabinets they were familiar with, used their own work documents as examples, and paired each with a confident peer. By the end of the session, all three were successfully creating folders, uploading documents, and sharing files - and became advocates for SharePoint in their departments.

Identifying Struggles Early

Context: Over 20 years of training, I've learned that people often struggle silently, especially in mixed-ability groups where they feel embarrassed to ask questions.

Action: I watch for non-verbal cues (confused expressions, hesitation, stopped note-taking), listen for the absence of questions (often more telling than their presence), observe hands-on practice attempts, and create safe spaces for people to admit confusion without judgment.

Outcome: This proactive approach means I can intervene before frustration builds, maintaining engagement and ensuring no one gets left behind - exactly what DofE needs when training diverse volunteers with varying technical confidence.

My Resolution Toolkit

Context: When I identify someone struggling, I have a systematic approach developed over thousands of training hours.

Action: I break concepts into smaller steps, provide alternative explanations using different approaches (visual, verbal, hands-on), offer discrete one-to-one support, pair struggling learners with confident peers, create additional practice exercises, and always verify understanding through practical application rather than just asking "does that make sense?"

Outcome: This toolkit ensures everyone succeeds regardless of their starting point, building confidence and competence that lasts beyond the training session - crucial for DofE's volunteer network who need to feel confident using systems like eDofE.

What strategies do you use to gather feedback on your training sessions and knowledge resources, and how do you apply it to improve future delivery?

Real Example: Regulatory Change Training Update

Context: After delivering training to a government department, I received feedback from staff in a particular role that they felt their point of view wasn't fully addressed because regulations had recently changed, so they were now doing different things from what was set out in the training.

Action: Rather than dismissing this as a minor issue, I scheduled a dedicated workshop with all staff in that role to learn about their new ways of working. I worked collaboratively with them to understand the regulatory changes and helped develop a new section for the training that accurately reflected their current responsibilities.

Outcome: We implemented this new section for the next training rollout, which resulted in much higher satisfaction scores from that role group and better practical application of the training content. This experience taught me the importance of staying current with regulatory changes and involving end-users in content development.

My Feedback Gathering Strategies

Context: Over 20 years, I've developed systematic approaches to gathering meaningful feedback that leads to actionable improvements.

Action: During sessions, I use real-time check-ins, observe engagement levels, and note questions or concerns. After sessions, I conduct post-training surveys, follow-up interviews, and track completion rates. Ongoing, I analyse support tickets for common issues, review knowledge base analytics, and maintain regular stakeholder check-ins.

Outcome: This multi-layered approach ensures I catch issues early and continuously improve training effectiveness - exactly what DofE needs to keep their volunteer training current and relevant as systems like eDofE evolve.

Turning Feedback Into Action

Context: Gathering feedback is only valuable if it leads to concrete improvements that benefit future learners.

Action: I take immediate actions (address urgent issues in next session, update materials), make iterative improvements (refine content based on patterns, adjust pacing), and implement strategic changes (redesign approaches, develop new resources). I always communicate improvements back to show feedback is valued and acted upon.

Outcome: This responsive approach builds trust with learners and stakeholders, ensures training stays relevant and effective, and creates a culture of continuous improvement - essential for DofE's dynamic environment supporting diverse volunteers.

How do you work with stakeholders, such as system developers or management, to ensure your training and knowledge resources align with organisational goals and system functionalities?

Real Example: Bespoke Chalet Booking System for Vulnerable Users

Context: I was developing a bespoke chalet booking system and needed to understand the app requirements alongside supporting resources that would best help a vulnerable user group enjoy a smooth holiday experience.

Action: I collaborated with a specialist Positive Behaviour team trained in working with this vulnerable group. Together, we travelled the country conducting one-to-one sessions and workshops to discover how best to develop the resources. Through this research, I learned that many users relied on Amazon smart home devices for web browsing and information access.

Outcome: This insight led me to create documents based on JSON architecture, which could easily integrate with Amazon software to deliver information in the format users needed. The result was a truly accessible booking system that met users where they were, rather than forcing them to adapt to traditional interfaces.

Cross-Functional Collaboration Approach

Context: Successful stakeholder collaboration requires understanding diverse perspectives and finding innovative solutions that serve everyone's needs.

Action: I work closely with system developers (early engagement, technical accuracy, feedback loops), management (strategic alignment, resource planning, regular reporting), and end users (needs assessment, co-creation, iterative testing). I maintain ongoing communication through regular meetings, shared documentation, and collaborative platforms.

Outcome: This multi-stakeholder approach ensures training and knowledge resources are technically accurate, strategically aligned, and genuinely useful for end users - exactly what DofE needs when supporting diverse volunteers with systems like eDofE.

Innovation Through User-Centered Design

Context: The chalet system project taught me that truly effective solutions often require thinking beyond conventional approaches.

Action: By travelling the country with specialists, conducting extensive user research, and discovering the Amazon smart home device usage pattern, I was able to design a solution that worked with users' existing habits rather than against them.

Outcome: This user-centered approach resulted in higher adoption rates and better user satisfaction. It's the same philosophy I'd bring to DofE - understanding how volunteers actually work and creating resources that support their natural workflows rather than forcing artificial processes.
